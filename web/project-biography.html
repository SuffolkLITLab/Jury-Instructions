I.          Problem Facing Legal Practitioner

a.      Question Presented

                       i.     Throughout this project, I tried to determine the precise legal issue that this program was trying to address. Although it would have been best to simply ask the client directly, between the coding hurdles and successes, unfortunately such an inquiry was not prioritized.

b.     Guiding Prediction as to Purpose and Specific Function

                       i.     While building this program, I tried to envision what the goal of prospective users would be and tried to adapt accordingly. I imagined that this program would primarily be used by legal scholars attempting to find any consistencies or discrepancies within jury instructions among jurisdictions across different states. Consequently, it was with this possible purpose in mind that I initially choose to make sure that the jury instructions used in the similarity program were all civil and not a mix of civil and criminal.

c.      Solution

                       i.     Giving the purpose proper consideration, one primary problem stood out to me – from a legal research perspective – and that was the often-decentralized nature of conducting such research. Even in the early stages of this project, I found it relatively difficult to find an efficient way to locate similar jury instruction topics across jurisdictions. The Internet Archive was a phenomenal resource for locating the different civil pattern jury instructions, but it was essentially not much different than walking into library with the goal of learning more about a narrow topic. Consequently, this program – in its future iterations – could possibly be a great source for individuals to research specific legal topics across jurisdictions.

II.        Research Conducted

a.      Legal Research

                       i.     Some cursory research was conducted in the early stages of this project to further understand the purpose and effect of jury instructions and the degree of latitude that the law allows for variation among jurisdictions. Since the goal of the program was to measure the similarities among states, it was important to have a baseline understanding of just how different the law allowed them to be. Beyond this, however, not much of the research conducted was directly related to the law.

b.     Technical and Coding Research

                       i.     Within the Internet Archive

1.     An initial hurdle that I had encountered was with finding a copy of a state’s jury instruction that was formatted in a workable way. Since most of the coding for the program was going to be done in Python, there had to be a jury instruction that would be amenable to that – in the sense that it could not have too many pieces of extraneous information in the file, like floating page numbers or incorrectly formatted text.

2.     Working with the client, I was given a list of civil pattern jury instructions that had been specifically uploaded for the purpose of coding the content into a program; a project that was being run concurrently with Harvard University. With this initial (and later intermediate) feedback, I was able to work with different jury instructions to see which would work best for the program.

                     ii.     Working Towards a Sustainable Baseline

1.     Despite my brief academic background in computer science, specifically in Python and HTML, there were certain tools within Python that I was not familiar with that were going to be necessary for the success of this project. Consequently, I had to do a fair amount of research regarding BeautifulSoup, for the purposes of parsing the text file, and spaCy, for the purposes of word vectorization. Additionally, I had to learn about cosine similarities and how the distance between words can be measured in a three-dimensional plane. Furthermore, specific tricks and tools that I learned in this course for regular expressions played a major role in the latter parts of this project. Finally, from an even more practical perspective, I learned how to use Anaconda-Navigator and Jupyter Notebook for all my coding needs.

III.      Current Model

a.      Expectations and Desired Outcome

                       i.     Following a demonstration with the client, there were a few things that were requested for the next iteration of the program. They are as follows:

1.     Add more jurisdictions

2.     Use the names of the sections instead of the numbers to delineate

3.     Create some type of readable table of content for the user

b.     Current MVP

                       i.     From these expectations/goals, I encountered quite a bit of obstacles but was able to create some viable workarounds for most of them, and the rest were positioned as works in progress.

1.     Regarding the addition of more jurisdiction, I was not able to complete the vectorization for any additional jurisdictions in time for the submission of this project. However, because I was taking a somewhat iterative approach, there are three different jurisdictions in the process of being parsed, Alabama, New York, and Ohio. Those notebooks are separate but are included in the GitHub repository.

2.     Using the names of the sections instead of the numbers to delineate each section topic ended up being a very large time commitment. Using different regex combinations, I attempted to capture a pattern of all capital letters of an undetermined length that are in between a number pattern and a word that starts with a capital letter followed by a lowercase letter [###.## XXX XX XXX Xx]. However, such a regular expression proved to be inviable, at least within the different approaches that I attempted.

a.      I do think that I found a somewhat satisfying work-around, at least within the short term and for the purposes of demonstration for this assignment. Realizing that the first 50-100 characters of each section for both jurisdictions will possess the section title, I created a new column in each dataframe that would essentially serve as a “preview”. Then in the similarity function, I called that column along with the numbered section head, so it now includes the hyperlinked section number and a short sample of what the section looks like. Although this is not explicitly what the client requested, I think it supports the intended purpose of the request – ease of use for a prospective user.

3.     The readable table of content was the most recent addition to this project but unfortunately it did not take the form that I would have wanted by the deadline. Similar to previewing the section titles for the similarity, I envisioned doing something comparable for the table. I was able to produce that aspect of the table, but my obstacles ended up laying in two places.

a.      The first was the formatting of the table. Since these jury instructions possess a large quantity of sections, the page for the user becomes quite long when scrolling through both the jury instruction similarity page and the table of content. For the table of content page, my goal was to make something more readable and that did not require the same amount of scrolling as the similarity page. However, I was not able to find a way to make that happen by the deadline.

b.     The second, and much more significant, was my inability to link the section numbers in the table of contents page to the anchor tags in the similarity page. The product for this page, as it currently stands, only links to the similarity page itself and not to the correct, corresponding section number. I have included the html page for the table of content, but I am not integrating it into the MVP.

VI.      Real World Viability

a.      Current

                       i.     At its current iteration, the program provides a fairly clear and readable proof of concept. Although it would have been better to incorporate more jurisdictions in the model to since a 1:1 similarity is different than a 1:1:1 comparison, the updated formatting does provide a clearer picture of what the program is meant to do. Additionally, given the computational cost of running the similarity on the entire jury instruction documents, only the first 50 sections of each could be produced for the deadline. If an additional jurisdiction were to be included, that number would likely have to be far less.

VII.    Sustainability

a.      A lot of the groundwork has been laid with this proof of concept so far, so there really would be much maintenance for the long-term once the issues in the short-term get sorted out. For instance, if more, or different, jurisdictions were to be added, they would just have to be properly parsed, placed into a dataframe, vectorized, and measured. This would not reverse any past similarities and could likely be done using similar methods.
